Inspired by The Devil Wears Prada

---

Andrea Sachs had never cared much about fashion.

That was the first thing everyone noticed about her when she walked into the towering glass building on Madison Avenue wearing a wrinkled blue sweater and shoes that squeaked slightly against the polished marble floor. The lobby itself felt colder than winter—silent people in black coats gliding past one another like they belonged to another species entirely.

Andrea clutched a folder to her chest and stared at the elevator numbers ticking upward.

She was twenty-three, fresh out of college, and desperate for a serious journalism job in New York City. Every newspaper she applied to had rejected her. Every magazine wanted experience she didn’t have. Rent was due in two weeks. Her tiny apartment smelled faintly of old radiator pipes and burnt toast.

So when someone told her that working for the editor-in-chief of Runway magazine could open every door in publishing, she ignored the fact that she had never willingly read a fashion magazine in her life.

The receptionist glanced up at her.

“You’re here for the assistant interview?”

Andrea nodded.

The woman looked her up and down once.

“Good luck.”

That should have been the warning.

---

The offices of Runway existed in a permanent state of panic.

Phones rang endlessly. Stylists hurried past carrying garment bags worth more than Andrea’s yearly rent. Editors argued about shades of white as if world peace depended on it.

Andrea sat nervously outside a large office with frosted glass walls.

Then the room changed.

Conversations lowered instantly. Chairs straightened. Someone nearly dropped a stack of fabric samples while rushing out of the hallway.

“She’s here,” whispered an assistant.

Miranda Priestly entered like a storm disguised as elegance.

Silver hair perfectly styled. Cream-colored coat draped over impossibly thin shoulders. Expression calm, controlled, terrifying.

She handed her bag to someone without looking at them.

“Why is this coffee lukewarm?”

Three people scattered immediately.

Andrea blinked.

Miranda walked into her office.

A few seconds later, another assistant hurried over. She wore sharp eyeliner and the exhausted expression of someone who hadn’t slept in years.

“You’re Andrea?” she asked.

“Yes.”

“I’m Emily. Listen carefully. Don’t speak unless she asks you something. Don’t fidget. Don’t smile too much. And if you value your life, never hand her a pen that doesn’t work.”

Andrea laughed weakly.

Emily didn’t.

“Oh no,” she said. “You think I’m joking.”

---

The interview lasted exactly nine minutes.

Miranda barely looked up from the papers on her desk.

“You have no style,” she said immediately.

Andrea shifted awkwardly. “I’m not really interested in fashion.”

“Clearly.”

Miranda flipped through her résumé.

“You’ve written for your college paper.”

“Yes.”

“And you believe that qualifies you to work at Runway?”

Andrea swallowed. “I’m smart. I learn fast.”

Miranda finally looked up.

Her eyes were pale and unreadable.

“Every girl wants this job.”

“I’m not every girl.”

Silence.

Then Miranda tossed the résumé onto the desk.

“Hire her,” she said to someone outside the office. “At the very least, she’ll be amusing.”

That was how Andrea became the junior assistant to the most feared woman in fashion publishing.

---

The first month nearly destroyed her.

Miranda demanded impossible things with complete confidence they could be done.

Flights changed during thunderstorms.

Unpublished manuscripts delivered overnight.

Coffee orders remembered exactly.

One morning Miranda called Andrea at 4:45 a.m.

“Where are the skirts from Calvin’s spring preview?”

Andrea sat upright in bed. “I—I didn’t know you needed them.”

“That’s because you fail to anticipate needs,” Miranda replied calmly. “A disappointing habit.”

Click.

Andrea stared at the phone in disbelief.

Her friends stopped seeing her.

Her boyfriend Nate complained she was always distracted. Even during dinner, Andrea checked her phone every thirty seconds in case Miranda called.

Emily mocked her constantly.

“You walk too slowly.”

“You answer phones like a substitute teacher.”

“Please stop wearing sweaters that look emotionally defeated.”

Andrea hated all of it.

And yet—

There was something magnetic about watching Miranda work.

During meetings, designers twice her age trembled while presenting collections. Photographers argued until Miranda quietly raised one eyebrow and ended the debate instantly.

She saw everything.

Hemlines. Fabrics. Trends months before anyone else noticed them.

At first Andrea thought fashion was shallow.

Then she realized it was power.

Power hidden beneath silk and perfume and impossible shoes.

---

One afternoon, Andrea laughed during a discussion about two nearly identical belts.

Miranda turned slowly.

“You think this is ridiculous.”

Andrea froze.

“No—”

“You believe fashion is unimportant because you personally lack interest in it.”

The office went silent.

Miranda stood and walked toward a rack of blue sweaters.

“This,” she said, touching Andrea’s sweater sleeve, “is not merely blue. It is cerulean.”

Andrea stared blankly.

Miranda continued.

“Years ago, designers introduced cerulean into their collections. Then it appeared in department stores. Then bargain bins. Eventually, someone selected this sweater for you believing it represented comfort and simplicity.”

She looked directly at Andrea.

“But the color was chosen for you by people in this industry. By people you dismiss.”

Nobody spoke.

Andrea suddenly felt very small.

After that day, she stopped laughing.

---

Slowly, she adapted.

She learned designers’ names.

She traded her bulky sweaters for fitted jackets and high boots.

Her hair changed. Her makeup sharpened. Even Emily admitted she looked “almost human now.”

People at Runway began treating her differently.

Doors opened.

Editors remembered her name.

Miranda trusted her with larger responsibilities.

For the first time since arriving in New York, Andrea felt successful.

But success came with a price.

Nate barely recognized her anymore.

“You cancel plans constantly,” he said one night.

“I’m working.”

“You’re always working.”

“You don’t understand how important this is.”

“And you don’t understand what it’s turning you into.”

Andrea rolled her eyes.

That became another thing she learned in the Runway world: how to dismiss people quickly.

Like Miranda did.

---

Paris Fashion Week arrived in February.

Emily had dreamed about attending for years. She prepared obsessively—strict diets, beauty treatments, schedules color-coded down to the minute.

Then she got sick.

Miranda called Andrea into the office.

“You’ll accompany me to Paris.”

Andrea’s stomach dropped.

“What about Emily?”

Miranda looked almost bored.

“She is unable to perform her duties.”

Andrea understood immediately.

Emily would be devastated.

“But she’s been preparing for months.”

“And?”

Andrea hesitated.

Miranda’s voice became colder.

“If your difficulty is guilt, I suggest you lose it quickly. The job requires priorities.”

Andrea accepted.

That night Emily cried in the office bathroom while pretending not to.

Andrea hated herself a little.

But not enough to refuse Paris.

---

Paris was beautiful and exhausting.

Runway moved through the city like royalty. Parties in gold-lit hotels. Models draped in diamonds. Designers whispering anxiously before runway shows.

Miranda remained untouchable through all of it.

But Andrea began noticing cracks.

Late-night phone calls.

Arguments behind closed doors.

Rumors that Miranda’s marriage was failing.

One evening Andrea entered the hotel suite to find Miranda sitting alone in silence, makeup removed, shoulders slumped with exhaustion.

For the first time, she looked human.

“My husband is leaving,” Miranda said quietly.

Andrea didn’t know what to say.

Miranda stared out the window at the Paris lights.

“Everyone wants to be us,” she murmured. “But they never understand what it costs.”

Andrea suddenly saw the loneliness hidden beneath the perfection.

The impossible standards.

The endless performance.

Miranda had built an empire so enormous that she could never truly rest inside it.

---

The next day, Miranda betrayed someone close to her to protect her own position at Runway.

Andrea watched it happen in real time.

A trusted colleague destroyed without warning.

Miranda remained calm throughout the entire thing.

“It’s business,” she said afterward.

Andrea realized then that Miranda would sacrifice anyone if necessary.

Friends. Assistants. Family.

Maybe even herself.

And the worst part?

Andrea understood why.

Power demanded constant feeding.

The moment weakness appeared, someone younger and hungrier waited to replace you.

That night Andrea walked alone through Paris.

She passed glowing cafés and couples laughing beneath streetlamps.

Her phone buzzed endlessly with work messages.

For once, she ignored them.

At a bridge overlooking the Seine, she stopped walking.

Then she removed her phone.

Looked at it for a long moment.

And threw it into the river.

---

Back in New York, Andrea quit Runway.

Miranda wasn’t surprised.

“You were never meant to belong here completely,” she said.

Andrea expected anger.

Instead, Miranda almost sounded disappointed.

“You have talent,” Miranda continued. “But you still want a life.”

Andrea nodded slowly.

“Yes.”

Miranda picked up another file.

“That’s the difference.”

Andrea left the office carrying a cardboard box with her belongings.

The same lobby that once intimidated her now felt strangely small.

Outside, New York roared around her—taxis, steam rising from the streets, people rushing nowhere and everywhere.

Her future remained uncertain.

But for the first time in months, she could breathe.

Weeks later, Andrea interviewed for a newspaper job.

The editor glanced at her résumé and paused at Runway magazine.

“You worked for Miranda Priestly?”

Andrea smiled faintly.

“Yes.”

The editor leaned back carefully.

“Then you can survive anything.”

And maybe that was true.

Because somewhere between the impossible demands, sleepless nights, beautiful clothes, and terrifying ambition, Andrea had changed.

Not into Miranda.

But into someone stronger than the girl who first walked into Runway wearing that old blue sweater.

Someone who finally understood that success meant nothing if you lost yourself trying to reach it.

And somewhere across the city, Miranda Priestly was already moving on to the next assistant, the next issue, the next impossible standard.

Unstoppable as ever.

Alone as ever.

Perfect as ever.
