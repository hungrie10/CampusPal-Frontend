Rain was already pouring by the time the school festival ended.

Students rushed through the gates laughing, screaming, trying not to get soaked while teachers yelled useless reminders about assignments nobody planned to do that weekend anyway.

Inside Classroom SS2-B, the lights buzzed weakly overhead.

Jay Okoro stacked leftover papers into neat piles at the teacher’s desk, still wearing his prefect badge even though school had technically ended an hour ago.

Of course he was still working.

Noah Adeyemi sat at the back of the class with his feet on another chair, scrolling through his phone like he owned the building.

“You know,” Noah said lazily, “normal people went home thirty minutes ago.”

Jay didn’t look up. “Normal people also helped clean after the festival.”

“Ah. So this is a charity event now.”

Jay sighed. “Why are you even here?”

“My bag.”

“It took you an hour to collect your bag?”

Noah finally stood, stretching slowly. Hoodie. Rings on his fingers. That permanently annoying half-smirk on his face.

Jay hated how calm he always looked.

“You ask too many questions,” Noah said.

Lightning flashed outside.

The lights flickered once.

Twice.

Then everything went black.

The entire building suddenly dropped into darkness.

Somewhere down the hallway, somebody shouted.

Then silence.

Jay froze. “You’ve got to be kidding me.”

Noah laughed softly in the dark. “Relax, prefect.”

“I am relaxed.”

“You sound like you’re about to cry.”

“I do not.”

Jay pulled out his phone flashlight immediately and pointed it around the room.

The beam landed directly on Noah.

And unfortunately, Noah looked unfairly good under dim lighting.

Wet curls falling slightly over his forehead. Sleeves rolled up. That stupid calm expression.

Jay quickly looked away.

“Generator should come back soon,” he muttered.

It didn’t.

Five minutes later, they discovered the hallway security doors had locked automatically because of the storm.

Ten minutes later, the teachers had all gone home already.

Twenty minutes later, both their phones had almost no signal.

“So,” Noah said, leaning against the classroom doorway, “we live here now.”

Jay rubbed his face. “This is not funny.”

“It’s a little funny.”

“It’s really not.”

Noah grinned.

And annoyingly, Jay almost smiled back.

—

By 8PM, the storm got worse.

Rain hammered the windows so hard it sounded like the school was underwater.

They’d searched almost the entire building already with their phone flashlights, but every exit was locked.

Jay sat on top of a desk near the windows, arms folded tightly.

Noah tossed him a bottle of juice from the festival leftovers.

“You’re stressfully quiet.”

“That’s not a phrase.”

“You know what I mean.”

Jay caught the drink automatically. “Thanks.”

Noah shrugged like it didn’t matter.

Silence settled between them.

Not awkward exactly.

Just… strange.

At school, they were always arguing. Competing. Teasing each other in class until teachers separated them.

Nobody ever left them alone together long enough for silence.

“You really don’t like me, huh?” Noah suddenly asked.

Jay blinked. “What?”

“You act like being near me physically hurts you.”

Jay scoffed immediately. “You’re dramatic.”

“You didn’t answer.”

Jay stared down at the unopened bottle in his hands.

“It’s not that I don’t like you,” he admitted quietly.

Noah raised an eyebrow. “Wow. Growth.”

“You’re just…” Jay struggled for words. “Annoying.”

“There it is.”

“And confusing.”

That made Noah pause.

“For somebody who acts like he doesn’t care,” Jay continued carefully, “you notice everything.”

The teasing expression on Noah’s face faded slightly.

The classroom suddenly felt smaller.

Rain crashed outside again.

“You notice things too,” Noah said softly.

Jay looked up.

Something shifted between them then.

Not obvious.

Not dramatic.

Just different.

And both of them noticed it immediately.

—

Around 10PM, they ended up sitting on the classroom floor sharing snacks stolen from the festival booths.

Noah had somehow convinced Jay to loosen his tie and stop acting like a stressed-out father of three.

“You know,” Noah said between bites of chips, “I genuinely thought you hated me when school started.”

Jay looked offended. “Why?”

“You looked at me like I committed crimes.”

“That’s because you kept sleeping in class.”

“I was tired.”

“You called the math teacher ‘bro.’”

“He was doing too much.”

Jay laughed before he could stop himself.

Noah stared at him.

Jay slowly realized he was still smiling.

“…What?”

“You laugh differently when it’s real.”

Jay’s stomach flipped unexpectedly.

“That sentence sounded illegal,” he muttered.

Noah grinned again, softer this time.

And for some reason, the room suddenly felt warmer.

—

By midnight, the storm had calmed slightly.

Their phones were nearly dead.

Jay sat near the windows while Noah lay across two desks staring at the ceiling.

“You ever get tired?” Noah asked quietly.

Jay frowned. “Of what?”

“Trying to be perfect all the time.”

Jay went still.

Nobody really asked him things like that.

Everyone usually assumed he had everything together.

Good grades. Prefect badge. Responsible. Calm.

But Noah looked at him like he could already see through all of it.

“Sometimes,” Jay admitted finally.

Noah nodded slowly like he understood.

“My parents think I’m wasting my life,” he said after a while.

Jay looked over immediately.

Noah laughed quietly, but there was no humor in it.

“They think I’m unserious. Lazy.” He shrugged. “Maybe they’re right.”

“You’re not lazy.”

Noah glanced at him.

“You literally notice every detail around you,” Jay said. “You remember things nobody else does. You act careless, but you’re not.”

The room became very quiet.

Noah sat up slowly.

Jay suddenly became hyper aware of how close they were.

“You really see me like that?” Noah asked softly.

Jay’s throat felt dry.

“Maybe.”

Neither of them moved.

Rain tapped gently against the windows now.

The air felt heavy.

Noah’s eyes dropped briefly to Jay’s lips before returning to his eyes again.

And that tiny movement changed everything.

Jay noticed it.

Noah noticed that he noticed.

And suddenly breathing felt harder than it should.

“You’re staring again,” Noah murmured.

Jay swallowed. “Maybe I like figuring you out.”

Noah smiled slowly.

Not teasing this time.

Something softer.

More dangerous.

Then—

BANG.

Both of them nearly jumped as the classroom door rattled loudly.

Security guards.

Finally.

The moment shattered instantly.

Jay stood too quickly, almost tripping over a chair while Noah laughed under his breath.

“You’re actually flustered,” Noah said.

“Shut up.”

“Oh my God. You are.”

Jay wanted the floor to swallow him alive.

—

The next Monday at school was unbearable.

Because now they knew something.

And pretending they didn’t felt impossible.

Every glance lasted too long.

Every joke felt loaded.

Their classmates noticed immediately.

“You two are being weird,” one girl said during lunch.

“We’re always weird,” Noah answered casually.

But under the table, Jay accidentally brushed against Noah’s hand.

And neither of them moved away.

—

Then came the rumors.

Because of course there were rumors.

Somebody saw them leaving the school together Saturday morning looking exhausted and wearing the same hoodie because Jay had gotten cold during the storm.

And secondary school students treated gossip like an Olympic sport.

By Wednesday, half the school thought they were secretly dating.

Jay was ready to evaporate from embarrassment.

Noah, unfortunately, thought the situation was hilarious.

“You’re smiling,” Jay accused during lunch.

“You’re cute when you’re angry.”

Jay choked on his drink.

Noah looked way too pleased with himself.

“See?” Noah said. “That reaction exactly.”

“You are actually impossible.”

“And yet you keep talking to me.”

Jay opened his mouth.

Closed it again.

Because annoyingly… Noah was right.

Somewhere between getting trapped overnight in a classroom and late-night conversations and shared silence and lingering looks—

Something happened.

Something neither of them knew how to name yet.

But they both felt it.

Every single time their shoulders brushed in crowded hallways.

Every time Noah waited outside Jay’s last class without admitting he was waiting.

Every time Jay smiled differently around him.

And honestly?

The entire school noticed before they did.
